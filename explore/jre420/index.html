Classical Viewpoint

    • High degree of specialization, coordination

Taylor’s Scientific Management

  • Clearly defined procedures with written instructions
  • Standardization, from research, allows for maximum efficiency
  • Functional foremanship, ie. supervisors are specialized in particular functions

Weber’s Bureaucracy

  • Each member reports to a single superior, ie. strict chain of command
  • Rules, regulations, and procedures enforced to make sure tasks are completed regardless
  • Strong emphasis on impersonal technical skills, assigned duties according to technical competence, centralized power at the top

Human Relations 

    • The following are incompatible

 

  • Specialization & Need for growth and improvement -> employee alienation
  • Centralized power & Creative ideas (often from lower levels with direct contact) ->fail to learn from mistakes, absence of innovation and adaptation
  • Impersonal rules & Goals of organization -> aim for norm instead of maximum (“red tape” mentality)

Manager Roles & Activities
Intuition is used to identify problem, perform well-learned mental tasks fast, synthesize isolated pieces of info and data, and double-check analyses.

 

  • Routine communication (formal receiving and transmitting information): monitor, disseminator (send info on facts and preferences to others), spokesperson (sending messages to external environment).
  • Networking (establishing and maintaining interpersonal relations): figurehead, leader(select, mentor, reward, discipline), liaison(maintain horizontal contacts inside and outside the organization). 
  • Traditional management (planning, decision making, controlling), human resource management (motivate, reinforcement, training, staffing, conflict resolution): entrepreneur (transforms problems and opportunities into plans for approved changes), disturbance handler (deals with problems stemming from employee conflicts), resource allocator (how to deploy critical resources), negotiator (negotiations with other organizations or individuals).

 

 

Psychological characteristics of an individual (personality)--the set of relatively stable ones--influence the way they think, feel, and behave. Behaviour is influenced by both the individual’s predisposed characteristics (dispositional approach) +  (and) their personality, as well as the situation or setting they find themselves in (situational approach) --this is the interactionist approach. 

Five-Factor Model

    • Openness to experience. (Curious, Original, Wide variety of interests v.s. Practical, Routine, Conventional)
    • Conscientiousness. (Dependable, Responsible v.s. Disorganized, Impulsive)
    • Extraversion. (Sociable, Outgoing v.s. Quiet, Reserved)
    • Agreeableness. (Tolerant, Cooperative v.s. Critical, Uncooperative) 
    • Neuroticism/Emotional stability. (Anxious, Depressed, Unstable Moods v.s. Calm)

 

  • Personality tests: there exists discrepancies between a person’s results depending on whether they took it themselves, or a friend or family member took it for them. This could be because of how we present ourselves to different people (for example, introverts may appear shy in front of strangers, but can be outgoing with close friends). Results may have been skewed if they wished to be perceived a certain way (prioritizing other factors over accuracy). 

 

 

Evaluating Self-Awareness of the Individual

    • Locus of control: a set of beliefs of whether one’s behaviour is controlled mainly by internal or external forces 
    • Self-monitoring: the extent to which people observe and regulate themselves (appearance + behaviour) in social settings and relationships (checks and balances)

 

  • Self-esteem: degree of positive self-evaluation; related to behavioural plasticity (where low self-esteem tend to be more susceptible to external and social influences)
  • Affect (how they view the world, oneself, and others); related to emotional contagion where negative affect has a much stronger effect than positive, and emotional labour
    AFFECT can be:
    EMOTION (perhaps intense, time -> 0, short-term)
    MOOD (less intense, for a longer period of time)
  • Proactive personality (tendency to take personal initiative)
  • General Self-Efficacy (GSE, a motivational trait, belief in their own ability to succeed  in a variety of challenging situations) 

 

  • Core self-evaluations (self-esteem, GSE, locus of control, neuroticism make up these) are found to be related to job satisfaction over time.

Perception: select, organize, interpret, retrieve, and respond to information. 

PERCEIVER + TARGET + SITUATIONAL CONTEXT

  • End result: expectations
  • Process: Unfamiliar target encountered -> Openness to target cues -> Encounter familiar cues -> Target categorized -> Cue selectivity -> Categorization strengthened* (*as this becomes stronger, the perceiver actively ignores/distorts cues that violate initial perceptions, so it will take a good number of contradictions before one recategorizes the target, ie. overcome previous perception)
  • Social identity theory: (involves values, beliefs, develop, think, act, feel)
    • Personal (unique personal characteristics -> interests, abilities, traits)
    • Social (groups, are relational and comparative, tendency to perceive members of own category in a more positive and favorable way)

 

Perception is selecting, organizing, interpreting, retrieving, and responding to information. 3 main roles: perceiver, target, and situational context.

Experience, motivational state (ie. needs), emotional state (perceptual defence) and ambiguity can influence perception. 

  • Selective: see what you want to see (such as, what stands out eg. liking another)
  • Primacy effect (early cues) v.s. Recency effect
  • Organize around central tendency (traits)
  • Implicit personality theories (what goes together)
  • Projection (attribute own traits to others)
  • Stereotype (ignore variations amongst others)
  • Signalling theory ( applicants -> recruitment process -> organization’s characteristics)
  • Contrast effect (earlier candidates affect later candidates’ impressions)
  • Leniency, harshness
  • Halo effect (rating based on other characteristics of the individual)
  • Similar-to-me effect (favorable evaluation given to those who resemble you)

There are also biases in attribution. 

  • Fundamental attribution error: overemphasis of influence of personality, failure to realize behaviour is distinctive to a particular situation
  • ACTOR-OBSERVER EFFECT: (hypocrisy, ie. you doing it v.s. Others doing the same thing)
  • SELF-SERVING BIAS: take credit for successful outcomes, deny responsibility for failures (explanation based on what happens after behaviour occurred), forget to give others credit, don’t learn from mistakes
